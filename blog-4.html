What Aerospace Taught Me About Banking Transformation

SS
Suman Sinha · PMP®
December 2025 9 min read Cross-Industry Career Governance

When I moved from aerospace into financial markets infrastructure, the assumption from some corners was that the transition would be steep. Different regulatory environment, different technology landscape, different stakeholder culture, different everything. And there is truth in that — the surface-level differences between a Honeywell aerospace operation and an LSEG financial markets programme are real and significant.

What surprised me, however, was how much transferred. Not in the sense of terminology or technical domain knowledge — that genuinely does not cross over — but at the level of delivery fundamentals. The patterns that determine whether a complex programme succeeds or fails are remarkably consistent across industries. What changes is the context in which those patterns play out. What does not change is the pattern itself.

Fifteen years and four industries later — aerospace, financial markets, banking transformation, and enterprise technology — that observation has only deepened. Cross-industry experience is not just a CV talking point. It is a genuine cognitive asset, and understanding why requires being specific about what actually transfers and what does not.

"The surface differences between industries are real. But the patterns that determine whether a complex programme succeeds or fails are remarkably consistent. What changes is the context. What does not change is the shape of the problem."

What Aerospace Builds Into You

Aerospace operations at the programme and portfolio level are defined by a particular kind of discipline. Safety-critical environments do not tolerate ambiguity about ownership, process, or sign-off authority. Everything is documented. Deviations from plan are not just noted — they are formally assessed, risk-rated, and resolved through a defined change process. The culture is built around the assumption that small oversights compound into large failures, because in safety-critical manufacturing and supply chains, they do.

This shapes how you think about governance in ways that are not always immediately obvious when you are inside it. You begin to see governance not as an administrative overhead but as an error-correction mechanism — a set of structured checkpoints that exist to catch problems before they propagate. You develop an instinct for the difference between process that protects delivery and process that merely consumes time. And you learn to build RAID logs, dependency maps, and escalation paths that are genuinely maintained rather than produced once and filed.

You also develop a particular relationship with financial control. Aerospace programmes are capital-intensive, long-cycle, and held to tight budget disciplines — earned value, cost-to-complete, variance analysis at workstream level. Managing this rigorously from early in a career builds habits around financial governance that translate directly into portfolio management in any industry.

The foundational transfer

Aerospace teaches you that governance is not bureaucracy — it is discipline in service of outcomes. That distinction, once internalised, changes how you design and run PMOs in every industry that follows.

Where the Patterns Repeated in Banking

When I arrived in financial markets and banking transformation, the specific domain was new — SWIFT messaging standards, regulatory capital requirements, data migration complexity, the particular stakeholder dynamics of a heavily regulated sector. None of that came from aerospace. What did come across was a set of pattern-recognition instincts that proved immediately useful.

The patterns that transferred directly

01

Dependency Failures Look the Same Everywhere

Whether it is a supplier component delivery in an aerospace supply chain or a data feed from a upstream system in a financial markets migration, the failure mode is identical — a dependency slips, the downstream impact is underestimated, the programme absorbs the delay silently until it can no longer do so. The mitigation discipline is also identical: actively managed dependency logs, explicit ownership, and early-warning triggers built into governance cadences rather than discovered in steering committee.

02

Scope Creep Has a Universal Signature

In aerospace, scope creep typically arrives as engineering change requests — small additions that each appear reasonable in isolation but cumulatively displace schedule and budget. In banking transformation, it arrives as stakeholder requirements that expand during build, or as regulatory interpretations that are refined after the programme has already committed to a design. The signature is the same: individually justifiable additions that collectively undermine the programme. The defence is also the same: a formal change process with explicit cost and schedule impact assessment, visible to the steering committee, before any addition is accepted.

03

Stakeholder Resistance Follows Predictable Shapes

Cross-functional programmes in aerospace — those touching engineering, supply chain, finance, and quality simultaneously — surface a predictable pattern of functional resistance. Each function has its own priorities, its own definition of success, and its own instinct to protect its domain. Banking transformation programmes are structurally identical in this regard. The functions are different — technology, operations, compliance, front office — but the dynamics of cross-functional friction, competing priorities, and the need for deliberate stakeholder management follow the same contours.

04

Governance Cadence Determines Programme Velocity

One of the clearest lessons from aerospace is that the rhythm of governance — how frequently decisions are made, how quickly issues are escalated, how transparently blockers are surfaced — sets the pace of the programme as much as the plan itself. A programme with a well-structured weekly governance cycle and a clear escalation path will consistently outperform one with better resources but slower decision-making. This held true in every banking and financial markets programme I subsequently worked on. Governance cadence is a delivery lever, not an overhead.

05

The Last 20% Is Always the Hardest

In aerospace programme delivery, there is a well-understood phenomenon where the final phase — integration, testing, sign-off, and handover — consistently consumes a disproportionate share of time and resource relative to plan. This is not a planning failure. It is a structural feature of complex delivery: the final phase is where all accumulated ambiguity, unresolved dependencies, and deferred decisions surface simultaneously. Banking transformation programmes, particularly those involving data migration, system cutover, and regulatory sign-off, exhibit exactly the same compression. Planning for this explicitly — building buffer into the final phase rather than distributing it evenly — is a lesson I carried directly from aerospace into every subsequent programme.

What Does Not Transfer — and Why That Matters

Being honest about the limits of transferability is as important as recognising what crosses over. Domain knowledge does not transfer, and it should not be assumed to. The regulatory framework of a banking programme — its specific compliance obligations, its relationship with conduct regulators, its technology architecture — requires genuine domain learning. Cross-industry experience is not a shortcut past that learning. It is a foundation that makes the learning faster and the pattern recognition more acute.

The cross-industry PM who understands this distinction brings something genuinely valuable to a new sector: a rigorous delivery framework, a pattern-recognition library built across multiple contexts, and a degree of objectivity about how things could be done differently — precisely because they have seen them done differently elsewhere. But that value depends on intellectual humility about what still needs to be learned.

"Cross-industry experience is not a shortcut past domain learning. It is a foundation that makes the learning faster and the pattern recognition more acute."

The Specific Value of Pattern Recognition at Scale

There is one advantage of cross-industry delivery experience that I have come to value above the others, and it is harder to articulate than the specific transferable skills. It is the ability to recognise a governance failure — or a delivery risk, or a stakeholder dynamic — at an earlier stage than someone who has only seen it in one industry context.

When you have seen a particular failure pattern play out across aerospace supply chain programmes and LSEG migration workstreams and Capgemini banking transformation portfolios, you develop an earlier-warning instinct for that pattern. You recognise the shape of it before it has fully formed. A dependency map that looks thin in a particular area. A stakeholder alignment conversation that is being deferred too many times. A risk register that has not moved in four weeks despite a delivery environment that is clearly changing. These are signals, and the ability to read them comes from having seen the same signal — in different clothing — across different industries over many years.

That is the real compounding return of a cross-industry career. Not just the sum of the skills from each sector, but the pattern recognition that emerges from having held all of them simultaneously — and having learned, industry by industry, what the shape of a complex delivery problem looks like before it becomes a crisis.
