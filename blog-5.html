How AI and LLMs Are Changing Requirements Gathering

SS
Suman Sinha · PMP®
April 2026 10 min read AI Project Delivery Requirements

Requirements gathering is where most projects quietly begin to go wrong. Not in a dramatic way — no one declares failure at a kickoff meeting. But the seeds of missed deadlines, budget overruns, and frustrated stakeholders are very often planted in the early weeks, when the team is still trying to understand what it is actually being asked to build.

I have seen this pattern repeat across industries — aerospace, financial markets, banking technology, enterprise systems. The domain changes. The stakeholders change. But the root cause is usually the same: requirements that were ambiguous, incomplete, or never properly agreed. By the time that becomes visible, the project is already in trouble.

AI and large language models (LLMs) are starting to change this. Not by replacing the people involved, but by bringing a level of rigour and consistency to the requirements process that manual methods rarely achieve in practice. This post looks at why the traditional approach breaks down, and what AI can genuinely do to improve it.

"The seeds of project failure are usually planted in the earliest weeks — when requirements are still being gathered, and ambiguity is still being treated as normal."

Where the Manual Process Falls Short

Requirements gathering done the traditional way — workshops, interview notes, email threads, spreadsheets, Word documents — is a surprisingly fragile process. The problems are rarely caused by a lack of effort. They are structural. The way information is collected, stored, and communicated creates gaps almost by design.

The common failure modes

01

Ambiguous Language That Everyone Reads Differently

Phrases like "the system should be fast," "users need flexibility," or "the report should be comprehensive" appear in requirements documents all the time. Each stakeholder who wrote those words had something specific in mind. The developers who read them had something different in mind. No one realises the mismatch until the build is done and the review meeting turns awkward.

02

Requirements That Live in Different Places

In most projects, requirements are scattered — some in workshop notes, some in emails, some in presentation decks from the business sponsor, some in informal conversations that were never written down at all. Pulling this into a single coherent baseline is time-consuming manual work, and something important nearly always gets missed.

03

Stakeholder Knowledge That Stays in People's Heads

Subject matter experts often know things that are deeply relevant to what the project needs — edge cases, exceptions, regulatory constraints, process quirks. But extracting that knowledge is hard. A one-hour workshop does not surface fifteen years of operational experience. A lot of what matters gets left out simply because no one knew the right questions to ask.

04

No Systematic Check for Conflicts and Gaps

When requirements come from multiple stakeholders across different functions, they often contradict each other. The finance team wants one thing. The operations team wants something incompatible. The compliance team adds constraints that neither knew about. Manually reviewing a large requirements set for internal conflicts is laborious and inconsistent — and reviewers who are close to the material tend to read what they expect to see, not what is actually there.

05

No Traceability Between Requirements and Delivery

Even when requirements are well-written, organisations often lose track of which requirements drove which design decisions, which test cases cover which requirements, and which changes to scope were formally agreed. When something goes wrong at the end — or when a stakeholder raises a concern in UAT — there is no clean audit trail to refer back to.

06

Version Control Is Usually an Afterthought

Requirements change. That is normal. What is not normal — but very common — is discovering late in a project that different team members have been working from different versions of the requirements document without knowing it. Manual version management is error-prone, and the cost of working from stale requirements compounds quickly.

The real cost

The Standish Group has tracked project outcomes for decades. Poor or unclear requirements consistently rank among the top causes of project failure — contributing to delays, cost overruns, and delivered systems that do not match what the business actually needed. This is not a new problem. But it is one that AI is now meaningfully equipped to help solve.

What AI and LLMs Bring to the Table

AI tools — particularly LLMs — are well-suited to the specific challenges of requirements work because they are good at exactly the things that humans find tedious and inconsistent: reading large volumes of text and identifying patterns, checking for gaps and contradictions, asking follow-up questions, and converting unstructured input into structured, usable output.

This is not about automating the requirements process and removing people from it. Stakeholder knowledge, business context, and judgement about priorities cannot be automated. But the supporting work — the capture, organisation, quality checking, and documentation — can be handled far better with AI than without it.

The specific benefits

01

Structured Elicitation Through Intelligent Questioning

LLMs can be configured to conduct structured requirements elicitation conversations with stakeholders, asking follow-up questions that a busy BA might not think to ask in a time-pressured workshop. When a stakeholder says "the system needs to be flexible," the AI can immediately probe: flexible in what way, for which users, under which conditions, with what constraints? This surfaces depth that interviews routinely miss.

02

Automatic Consolidation From Multiple Sources

LLMs can ingest workshop transcripts, email threads, presentation decks, legacy documents, and process maps, then synthesise them into a clean, structured requirements register. What would take a BA several days of manual work can be produced in minutes — with sources cited so that every requirement can be traced back to its origin.

03

Ambiguity Detection and Plain-Language Rewriting

AI tools can scan a requirements document and flag every statement that is vague, unmeasurable, or open to interpretation. They can then suggest specific rewrites: turning "the system should respond quickly" into "the system must return search results within two seconds for 95% of queries under normal load." This alone eliminates a significant proportion of the misunderstandings that typically surface during build or testing.

04

Conflict and Contradiction Detection

Given a full set of requirements, an LLM can systematically check for internal conflicts — places where two requirements are incompatible, or where a constraint from one stakeholder contradicts an assumption from another. This kind of cross-referencing is exactly what manual review struggles with at scale, and exactly what AI handles well.

05

Gap Analysis Against Known Standards and Prior Projects

LLMs trained on regulatory frameworks, industry standards, and prior project documentation can identify missing requirements that would not be obvious to someone focused on the current project in isolation. In regulated industries — banking, healthcare, aerospace — this capability is particularly valuable. The AI can flag: "This type of system typically requires a data retention policy. I do not see one in the current requirements."

06

Automatic Traceability Matrix Generation

Once requirements are structured, AI tools can automatically generate and maintain a traceability matrix linking each requirement to design decisions, test cases, and change requests. This removes the manual overhead that makes traceability matrices so often out of date in practice — and gives the project a genuine audit trail throughout delivery.

07

Consistent Documentation, Regardless of Who Contributes

In large projects, requirements documentation is often produced by multiple people with different styles, levels of detail, and interpretations of what "a well-written requirement" looks like. AI can normalise this — ensuring every requirement is structured to the same standard, with consistent terminology, measurement criteria, and format. The resulting document reads as if one disciplined person wrote it.

08

Real-Time Translation for Diverse Stakeholder Groups

Business stakeholders and technical teams often describe the same need in entirely different language. AI tools can bridge this gap in real time — translating a business stakeholder's operational description into a technically precise specification, and back again for review. This reduces the back-and-forth cycles that slow down requirements sign-off considerably.

Beyond Requirements: Planning, Build and Testing

The benefits of AI-assisted requirements work do not stop once the requirements document is signed off. The same structured, traceable, unambiguous requirements baseline that AI helps create also makes every downstream phase better.

Phase 01

Planning

Well-structured requirements make estimation more reliable. AI tools can analyse a requirements set and flag high-complexity items, missing dependencies, and scope areas that typically cause planning assumptions to break. They can also generate initial work breakdown structures from requirements, giving the project a faster start on scheduling. When requirements change during planning, AI can immediately show the downstream impact — which tasks are affected, which dependencies shift, and how the critical path moves.

Phase 02

Build

During development, teams can query the requirements baseline directly — asking an AI assistant whether a particular edge case is covered, or which requirement drives a specific design decision. This reduces the time developers spend hunting through documents and the informal interpretations they make when they cannot find an answer. Change requests can also be assessed automatically against the requirements baseline: what does this change affect, what needs to be re-tested, and what is the scope impact?

Phase 03

Testing

With a clean traceability matrix, AI can generate test case outlines directly from requirements — ensuring every requirement has test coverage and that nothing is left to chance. During UAT, AI can help map defects and feedback back to the specific requirements they relate to, making triage faster and more structured. And because the requirements are unambiguous, there is less room for the arguments about "what we agreed" that typically slow down the testing phase.

The compounding effect

The quality of requirements work has a compounding effect on every phase that follows. A project with clean, traceable, unambiguous requirements at the start will plan better, build faster, and test more reliably than one that carries ambiguity through from the beginning. AI does not just improve requirements gathering in isolation — it improves the entire delivery chain.

A Practical Reality Check

It would be misleading to suggest that AI solves requirements problems automatically. The tools require good inputs — if stakeholders are not willing to engage thoughtfully, or if the source documents are genuinely incomplete, AI cannot manufacture substance that is not there. The technology amplifies what exists; it does not replace it.

There are also genuine concerns worth taking seriously. Sensitive requirements — those involving personal data, financial information, or proprietary processes — need careful handling before they go near any external AI system. Most organisations will need to think carefully about what they share, where, and under what governance. Private deployments, where the organisation controls the model and the data, address many of these concerns, but require investment.

The most effective approach treats AI as a rigorous, tireless collaborator that handles the structural and analytical work — freeing up the people involved to focus on the judgement, negotiation, and relationship management that genuinely requires human input.

"AI does not replace the work of understanding what a project actually needs. It removes the structural noise that prevents that understanding from being captured accurately."

What This Means For Project Managers

For PMs and PMO leaders, this is not a technology question in disguise. It is a delivery quality question. The organisations that adopt AI-assisted requirements practices will, over time, start projects with better baselines, make fewer costly assumptions, and carry less ambiguity into their planning and build phases. Those that do not will continue to absorb the same structural problems in their delivery performance.

The starting point does not need to be complicated. Most teams can begin with a single AI-assisted review of their next requirements document — checking for ambiguity, gaps, and contradictions. That one step, done consistently, already changes the quality of what goes into delivery. Everything beyond that is incremental from a foundation that is already better.

The bottom line

Requirements quality is a delivery lever that most organisations under-invest in, because the failure costs are deferred and diffuse. AI gives teams a practical, accessible way to raise that quality systematically — not as a transformation programme, but as a better way of doing work that was always worth doing well.
